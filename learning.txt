## Language Models are Few-Shot Learners + Brown et al. + 2020
This paper establishes that scaling language models improves in-context learning, with few-shot performance approaching fine-tuned SOTA on many tasks without gradient updates. The gap between zero/one/few-shot performance grows with model size, suggesting larger models are better meta-learners. Key gaps remain: NLI tasks (ANLI) and complex reasoning (RACE) still struggle, and data contamination requires careful monitoring.

## Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them + Lu et al. + 2022
This paper reveals that prompt order sensitivity is a fundamental issue in in-context learning that persists even at 175B parameters—contradicting any assumption from Brown et al. that scaling alone resolves few-shot challenges. The entropy-based probing method cleverly exploits the LM's generative nature to construct an artificial development set, achieving 13% relative improvement without requiring held-out labels. A critical gap emerges: sentence-pair tasks (RTE, CB) remain near random even with optimal ordering for smaller models, suggesting some tasks lack performant prompts entirely regardless of design.

## Large Language Models Understand and Can Be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli + Li et al. + 2023
This paper introduces an interdisciplinary approach by injecting psychological emotional stimuli into prompts, achieving 8% improvement on simple tasks and 115% on hard BIG-Bench tasks—suggesting that emotional framing may help LLMs tackle challenging reasoning more than straightforward tasks. The finding that larger models and higher temperature settings benefit more from EmotionPrompt complements Lu et al.'s observation that scaling alone doesn't resolve prompt design challenges, but emotional scaffolding might. A notable gap: the mechanism remains opaque (attention weights on positive words explain ~50-70% of contribution), leaving open how exactly emotional stimuli alter LLM computation versus simply biasing output toward more confident/deliberative language.

## How Robust are LLMs to In-Context Majority Label Bias + Gupta et al. + 2023
This paper challenges prior assertions (Zhao et al. 2021) that LLMs lack robustness to majority label bias, revealing that larger models achieve 80-100% robustness on binary tasks—contradicting any assumption that in-context learning is inherently fragile to label distribution skew. The RobustnessBoundary@K metric cleverly quantifies fault tolerance across distributional settings, showing that model size and task-specific instructions both contribute to robustness, with larger models paradoxically more sensitive to instruction presence (~28% drop without instruction for 30B/40B vs ~8% for 7B). A critical gap emerges for multi-class tasks (~50% robustness), suggesting that while scaling helps binary classification resist bias, complex label spaces remain vulnerable regardless of model size.

## Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs + Gupta et al. + 2024
This paper exposes a disturbing contradiction: while LLMs overtly reject stereotypes when asked directly ("Are disabled people worse at math?"), they manifest stereotypical presumptions when prompted to embody personas, resulting in 33-70% performance drops on reasoning tasks. The bias manifests dually—explicit abstentions citing limiting assumptions ("As a physically-disabled person, I cannot do math") and implicit reasoning errors on shared non-abstained questions—suggesting alignment techniques like RLHF only address surface-level expressions while deep-rooted biases persist. A critical gap emerges: simple de-biasing prompts fail entirely, and task-specific expertise augmentation lacks generalizability for open-ended conversations, implying that persona-induced bias requires architectural or training interventions rather than prompt engineering. This complements Gupta et al. (2023)'s finding that scaling improves robustness to majority label bias but reveals that scaling alone doesn't resolve socio-demographic stereotyping—GPT-4-Turbo still shows bias in 42% of personas.
