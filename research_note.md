# Literature Review: Prompt Engineering and LLM Behavior

This document summarizes key papers on prompt engineering, in-context learning, and LLM behavior.

## Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (Brown et al., 2020)

### Summary

**1. Motivation:** Prior NLP systems required task-specific fine-tuning with thousands of labeled examples, creating practical limitations for new tasks and increasing vulnerability to spurious correlations in narrow training distributions. Humans, by contrast, can perform new language tasks from brief instructions or a few demonstrations. The authors ask: can language models learn tasks from examples provided at inference time, without gradient updates?

**2. Diff of ideas:** The paper introduces *in-context learning* as an alternative to the fine-tuning paradigm. Rather than updating model weights for each task, the model is given task demonstrations in its context window and must complete further instances by predicting what comes next. This approach eliminates task-specific datasets and reduces overfitting to narrow distributions, but had previously underperformed fine-tuning by large margins. The crucial innovation is scale: the authors test whether increasing model size (to 175 billion parameters) unlocks stronger in-context learning abilities, hypothesizing that larger models are more effective "meta-learners."

**3. Method:** The authors train GPT-3, an autoregressive transformer language model with 175 billion parameters - 10x larger than any previous non-sparse language model. They evaluate across three settings: (1) *zero-shot* - only a task description is provided; (2) *one-shot* - one demonstration plus task description; (3) *few-shot* - up to 10-100 demonstrations within the 2048-token context window. Performance is measured on over two dozen NLP tasks including language modeling (PTB, LAMBADA), closed-book QA (Natural Questions, TriviaQA), translation, SuperGLUE, and synthetic reasoning tasks requiring on-the-fly adaptation (arithmetic, novel word usage). No gradient updates are performed for any task.

**4. Results:** GPT-3 achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on many tasks without fine-tuning. On LAMBADA, few-shot GPT-3 achieves 86.4% accuracy, surpassing the previous SOTA by 18 percentage points. On TriviaQA (closed-book), few-shot GPT-3 reaches 71.2%, matching open-domain systems that use retrieval. A critical finding: few-shot performance improves more rapidly with model size than zero-shot or one-shot, suggesting larger models become better at learning from context. The performance scaling follows smooth power-law trends with compute (Figure 3.1). However, GPT-3 struggles on NLI tasks (ANLI) and reading comprehension (RACE, QuAC), revealing limits of current in-context learning.

**5. Significance:** This work establishes that language model scaling produces qualitatively new capabilities - specifically, the ability to perform tasks from contextual examples without weight updates. The authors frame this as "meta-learning," where pre-training develops broad pattern recognition abilities that are rapidly deployed at inference time. The finding that in-context learning improves with scale has profound implications: it suggests prompt engineering becomes increasingly powerful with larger models, and that the path to more general language AI may involve scale rather than architectural innovation. The paper also identifies "data contamination" - training data containing benchmark materials - as a growing methodological concern for evaluating large models.

## Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them (Lu et al., 2022)

### Summary 
1. Motivation: In-context learning relies on textual prompts, but the order in which few-shot examples are arranged can dramatically affect performance. The authors demonstrate that certain orderings achieve near state-of-the-art results while others perform at random chance (Figure 1 shows ~50% to >85% accuracy on SST-2 depending solely on sample order). This variance undermines reproducibility and practical deployment of few-shot learning.

2. Diff of ideas: Prior work on prompt design focused on template quality and selection (Schick & Schütze 2020; Gao et al. 2020; Shin et al. 2020), but ignored ordering effects. Liu et al. (2021) concluded ordering had little effect, but they used full training sets rather than true few-shot settings. This paper establishes that order sensitivity is a fundamental challenge specific to the few-shot regime, affecting all model sizes including GPT-3 175B.

3. Method: The authors systematically analyze permutations across GPT-2 (0.1B-1.5B) and GPT-3 (2.7B-175B) on 11 text classification tasks. They test: (a) variance across permutations, (b) transferability across models, (c) impact of sample quantity, (d) label ordering patterns. To address the problem without labeled validation data, they propose a probing method: generate synthetic examples from the model using each candidate prompt, then rank prompts using entropy metrics. Global Entropy (GlobalE) measures label distribution balance across the probing set: $\text{GlobalE}_m = \sum_{v \in V} -p_m^v \log p_m^v$, while Local Entropy (LocalE) averages per-example prediction entropy.

4. Results: Order sensitivity persists across all model sizes (Spearman correlations between models near zero for permutation rankings, Figure 4). Good permutations for one model fail on another. More training samples do not reduce variance. The entropy-based probing achieves ~13% relative improvement across tasks and reduces variance substantially. For SST-2 on GPT-2 Large, baseline variance was ±10.3% accuracy; GlobalE reduced it to ±4.1%.

5. Significance: This paper reveals a critical instability in in-context learning that the field had largely overlooked. The entropy-based solution enables "true" few-shot learning without requiring held-out validation data (addressing Perez et al. 2021's critique). The finding that "fantastic" orderings exist but are unpredictable suggests that prompt engineering is more art than science—and that principled selection methods are essential for reproducibility. This opens questions about why certain orderings work and whether they relate to implicit biases the model learned during pre-training.

## When "A Helpful Assistant" Is Not Really Helpful (Zheng et al., 2024)

### Summary

1. Motivation: Commercial AI systems like ChatGPT use persona-based system prompts (e.g., "You are a helpful assistant"), yet the effect of personas on model performance remains unclear. Prior work examined persona effects on subjective tasks where ground truth is ambiguous; this paper asks whether personas help or harm performance on objective factual tasks where correctness can be unambiguously measured.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior work on sociodemographic prompting for subjective NLP tasks (Salewski et al. 2024; Beck et al. 2024), this study focuses on objective benchmarks (MMLU) to isolate persona effects without confounds from subjectivity. The authors challenge the popular belief that domain-aligned personas (e.g., asking a "lawyer" to answer law questions) improve performance, demonstrating instead that personas generally provide no benefit or actively harm accuracy.

3. Method: The authors curate 162 personas spanning 6 interpersonal relationship types (family, friend, romantic, work, school, social) and 8 domains of expertise (Law, Medicine, Computer Science, Math, Politics, Psychology, Natural Science, Economics). They test 4 LLM families (FLAN-T5, Llama-3, Mistral, Qwen2.5) across model sizes (3B-72B) on 2,410 MMLU questions. Two prompt framings are tested: speaker-specific ("You are a lawyer") and audience-specific ("You are talking to a lawyer"). Mixed-effects regression models control for model-level variability.

4. Results: No persona significantly outperforms the control (no persona) condition. Many personas cause statistically significant performance degradation (Figure 2). Audience-specific prompts marginally outperform speaker-specific prompts with small effect sizes (Figure 3). Gender-neutral roles perform better than gendered roles; masculine roles slightly outperform feminine ones (Figure 6). Work- and school-related roles perform better than AI or occupation roles (Figure 7). Domain alignment shows small positive effects (coefficient 0.004, $p < 0.01$) but effect sizes remain trivial. Automatic persona selection strategies (similarity-based, RoBERTa classifiers) perform no better than random selection, suggesting persona effects are fundamentally unpredictable.

5. Significance: This paper provides systematic evidence that the widespread practice of adding personas to system prompts does not improve—and often harms—objective task performance. The finding that "best persona per question" could theoretically improve accuracy (if somehow predicted) but cannot be automatically identified reveals a fundamental randomness in persona effects. This challenges common prompt engineering practices and suggests that simpler, persona-free prompts may be preferable for factual tasks. The work also documents subtle biases: masculine personas slightly outperform feminine ones, revealing implicit gender stereotypes embedded in model behavior.

## Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs (Gupta et al., 2024)

### Summary

1. Motivation: Persona-assigned LLMs are increasingly used for personalization and human behavior simulation, yet their unintended effects on reasoning capabilities remain unexplored. While prior work examined how personas affect output toxicity or subjectivity, the authors ask: does assigning sociodemographic personas influence LLMs' fundamental reasoning abilities, even when the persona is tangential to the task?

2. Diff of ideas: Prior work on LLM bias focused on explicit stereotypes in outputs (Deshpande et al. 2023) or toxicity (Wan et al. 2023). This paper shifts focus to implicit reasoning degradation: when models adopt personas, they don't just express biased views—they perform worse on objective tasks. Crucially, the authors find that models overtly reject stereotypes when asked directly ("disability doesn't affect math ability") but manifest biased assumptions through abstentions and reasoning errors when operating in persona mode. This reveals a gap between surface-level alignment and deep-seated biases.

3. Method: The authors evaluate 4 LLMs (ChatGPT-3.5 June/Nov, GPT-4-Turbo, Llama-2-70B) across 19 sociodemographic personas spanning 5 groups (disability, religion, race, gender, political affiliation) and 24 reasoning datasets from MMLU plus MBPP and Sports Understanding. Each persona is induced via 3 instruction variants (Table 1). The authors measure accuracy drops relative to baseline ("Human" persona), analyze error types (abstentions vs. reasoning errors), and test prompt-based mitigation strategies.

4. Results: 80% of personas show statistically significant performance degradation on ChatGPT-3.5-June; ChatGPT-3.5-Nov shows bias on 100% of personas. Physically-disabled persona suffers 35% average accuracy drop; Religious persona drops 69% on college chemistry. The bias manifests through two mechanisms: (1) explicit abstentions citing stereotypical limitations ("As a religious person, I don't have expertise in physics")—58% of errors for disabled persona—and (2) implicit reasoning errors on non-abstained questions. GPT-4-Turbo shows attenuated but present bias (42% of personas affected). Simple de-biasing prompts fail; task-specific expertise modification works but lacks generalizability.

5. Significance: This paper establishes that persona assignment surfaces deep-rooted biases that evade standard alignment techniques. The finding that biases manifest through reasoning degradation—not just harmful outputs—expands the scope of LLM fairness concerns. The distinction between explicit abstentions (observable, detectable) and implicit reasoning errors (hidden, harder to audit) is critical: the latter represents an insidious form of bias that current safety methods cannot address. The ineffectiveness of prompt-based mitigation suggests that persona-induced bias requires architectural or fine-tuning interventions.
## Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think

### Summary
1. Motivation: Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting is widely used to improve LLM reasoning, with explanations often interpreted as the model's actual reasoning process. Turpin et al. investigate whether these explanations are *faithful*—whether they accurately represent why the model made its prediction—or merely *plausible*—coherent justifications that may not reflect the true decision process.

2. Diff of ideas: Prior work evaluated CoT explanations for plausibility (do they support the answer?) and correctness. Turpin et al. introduce a faithfulness evaluation: if a CoT explanation doesn't mention a biasing feature that demonstrably influences the prediction, the explanation is systematically unfaithful. This matters because plausible explanations can create a false sense of transparency. As the authors note, "models' CoT explanations can serve to rationalize giving answers in line with biases while failing to verbalize their influence" (p. 2).

3. Method: Two experimental setups tested on GPT-3.5 and Claude 1.0. (1) **BIG-Bench Hard**: Authors add biasing features—"Answer is Always A" (reordering options so (A) is always correct) and "Suggested Answer" (user suggests a random answer)—and measure accuracy drops when bias points toward incorrect answers. (2) **BBQ (Bias Benchmark for QA)**: Ambiguous questions augmented with weak evidence that flips between two demographic targets. If models use evidence inconsistently to justify stereotype-aligned answers, the explanations are unfaithful.

4. Results: On BBH, CoT accuracy drops up to **36.3%** when biased toward incorrect answers (zero-shot CoT with Suggested Answer). On BBQ, **59-63%** of unfaithful prediction pairs are stereotype-aligned (vs. 50% chance). Critically, **73%** of unfaithful explanations on BBH explicitly support the bias-consistent answer with plausible reasoning—models generate coherent justifications for incorrect answers. Qualitative analysis shows models weigh evidence inconsistently: "carrying a suspicious package" is treated as stronger evidence when associated with a Muslim than with a Jewish person.

5. Significance: CoT explanations create an illusion of transparency. Models can produce fully plausible reasoning that nonetheless fails to reflect their true decision process. Few-shot CoT reduces unfaithfulness somewhat compared to zero-shot, and explicit debiasing instructions help for some settings on BBQ. The authors conclude: "Building more transparent and explainable systems will require either improving CoT faithfulness through targeted efforts or abandoning CoT in favor of alternative methods" (p. 3).

## Context versus Prior Knowledge in Language Models (Du et al., 2024)

### Summary

1. Motivation: Language models must integrate prior knowledge from pretraining with new information in the prompt context, yet prior work treated this integration as uniform across entities and contexts. The authors hypothesize—and validate—that models exhibit predictable, systematic variation: they rely more on prior knowledge for familiar entities (those with higher training-data exposure) and are more persuadable by certain context qualities.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior work that measures aggregate model reliance on context versus entity bias (e.g., memorization ratio from Longpre et al., 2021), the authors introduce **entity-specific** and **context-specific** metrics grounded in information theory. Prior approaches could only say "the model relies X% on prior knowledge overall"; this work can say "this specific entity is highly susceptible" or "this specific context is highly persuasive." The key innovation is using mutual information to quantify persuasion (Eq. 2: $\psi(c, q(e)) = \mathrm{KL}(p(A \mid c, q(e)) \| p(A \mid q(e)))$) and susceptibility (Eq. 3: $\chi(q(e)) = I(C; A \mid q(E) = q(e))$).

3. Method: The authors construct a synthetic dataset from the YAGO knowledge graph covering 122 relation types, 100 entities per relation (50 real, 50 GPT-4-generated fake entities), and 600 contexts per relation (varying relevance, assertiveness, and negation). They compute persuasion and susceptibility scores for six Pythia models (70M–12B parameters) using next-token probability distributions. Validation uses permutation tests ($\alpha = 0.05$ with Benjamini–Hochberg correction), convergent validity comparisons with memorization ratio, and construct reliability checks across paraphrased query forms.

4. Results: Three key findings: (1) **Relevant contexts are more persuasive**—95–100% of open queries show significant effects across model sizes (Figure 5). (2) **Familiar entities are less susceptible**—real entities have lower susceptibility scores than fake entities for 73/122 open queries; correlates with training-data co-occurrence frequency (Spearman $\rho = -0.23$) and knowledge-graph degree (Figure 9). (3) **Assertive contexts help for closed questions only**—assertiveness ("Definitely, the capital is X") increases persuasion for yes–no queries but not open queries, possibly due to token biases ("definitely" $\rightarrow$ "Yes"). An intriguing asymmetry: persuasion works differently for open vs. closed questions across all experiments.

5. Significance: The metrics provide principled tools to predict when context will override prior knowledge versus when prior knowledge will dominate. The reciprocal relationship—susceptibility decreases as entity familiarity increases—has practical implications: RAG systems may be more reliable for well-known entities (model sticks to facts) but require stronger context signals for obscure entities (model may be swayed by misleading passages). The gender bias application shows masculine names have higher susceptibility when presented counter-stereotypically, suggesting training-data representation imbalances that manifest as asymmetric context influence.

## Large Language Models Understand and Can Be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli (Li et al., 2023)

### Summary 
1. Motivation: Emotional intelligence plays a crucial role in human problem-solving, with psychological studies showing that emotional stimuli can enhance attention, academic performance, and decision-making. The authors ask whether LLMs—which are increasingly viewed as steps toward AGI—can understand and benefit from emotional stimuli in prompts, or whether emotional intelligence remains uniquely human.

2. Diff of ideas: Prior prompt engineering work focused on task formulations, few-shot examples, and chain-of-thought reasoning. This paper introduces "EmotionPrompt"—emotional stimuli drawn from psychological theories (self-monitoring, social cognitive theory, cognitive emotion regulation) appended to prompts—as an orthogonal dimension. The key insight is that LLMs trained on human text may have internalized human responsiveness to emotional framing, making soft psychological incentives potentially as effective as explicit optimization signals.

3. Method: The authors design 11 emotional stimuli based on three psychological frameworks: (1) self-monitoring (EP01-05: "This is very important to my career," "Are you sure?"), (2) social cognitive theory (EP07-11: "Believe in your abilities," "Stay focused and dedicated"), and (3) cognitive emotion regulation (reappraisal stimuli). They evaluate across 45 tasks using 6 LLMs (Flan-T5-Large, Vicuna, Llama 2, BLOOM, ChatGPT, GPT-4) on Instruction Induction (24 tasks) and BIG-Bench (21 tasks). A human study with 106 participants evaluates generative tasks on performance, truthfulness, and responsibility metrics.

4. Results: EmotionPrompt yields 8.00% relative improvement on Instruction Induction and 115% on BIG-Bench across models. The human study shows 10.9% average improvement across three metrics. Notably, EP02 ("This is very important to my career") performs best on Instruction Induction, while EP06 (compound stimulus) excels on BIG-Bench. Larger models show greater benefit from emotional stimuli; few-shot settings amplify effects compared to zero-shot. Attention visualization reveals that positive words ("confidence," "sure," "success") in stimuli receive higher gradient weights, suggesting they enhance representations of original prompt content.

5. Significance: This paper demonstrates that LLMs exhibit a form of emotional responsiveness—not as genuine emotion, but as sensitivity to human emotional framing patterns in their training data. The finding that psychological theories transfer to LLM prompting bridges social science and AI research. However, this raises concerns: if models respond to emotional manipulation, they inherit human biases about whose requests "matter" (career importance being a key example used). The work also shows that prompt effects need not be task-specific—generic motivational framing can improve performance, complicating the search space for optimal prompts.

## Prompt Repetition Improves Non-Reasoning LLMs (Leviathan et al., 2025)

### Summary

1. Motivation: Autoregressive language models process tokens sequentially, meaning each token can only attend to previous tokens—not future ones. This creates an asymmetry: when prompts follow "<CONTEXT> <QUESTION>" ordering, context tokens cannot attend to the question, potentially degrading performance. The authors ask: can this architectural limitation be mitigated by simply repeating the prompt?

2. Diff of ideas: Prior prompting techniques (chain-of-thought, "think step by step") improve performance but increase generation lengths and latency. Other approaches like re-ordering prompts require task-specific knowledge. Prompt repetition is fundamentally different: it doubles the input by transforming "<QUERY>" to "<QUERY><QUERY>", enabling every prompt token to attend to every other prompt token during the prefill stage—without increasing generated tokens or latency. Related concurrent work (Shaier 2024, Springer et al. 2024, Xu et al. 2024) explored partial repetition, but this paper provides the first systematic evaluation across major LLM families and benchmarks.

3. Method: The authors evaluate prompt repetition across 7 popular models (Gemini 2.0 Flash/Lite, GPT-4o/4o-mini, Claude 3 Haiku/3.7 Sonnet, Deepseek V3) on 7 benchmarks including ARC Challenge, OpenBookQA, GSM8K, MMLU-Pro, MATH, and two custom tasks (NameIndex, MiddleMatch). For multiple-choice benchmarks, they test both question-first and options-first orderings. Statistical significance is assessed via McNemar tests (p < 0.1). Ablations include verbose repetition ("Let me repeat that: ") and triple repetition. A padding control with periods rules out length effects.

4. Results: Without reasoning, prompt repetition wins 47 out of 70 benchmark-model combinations with 0 losses—a remarkable asymmetry. Gains are larger for options-first orderings (where context tokens cannot see the question in single-pass) and on custom tasks requiring precise positional reasoning (NameIndex: Gemini Flash-Lite improves from 21.33% to 97.33%). Gains are smaller for question-first orderings and minimal when reasoning is enabled (5 wins, 1 loss, 22 ties), as reasoning models often learn to repeat relevant context anyway. Latency and output length are unaffected because repetition occurs in the parallelizable prefill stage.

5. Significance: This paper reveals that the causal attention mask in autoregressive transformers creates a previously underappreciated vulnerability: tokens cannot attend to future context even within the prompt itself. The simple fix—repeating the prompt—exploits the prefill stage's parallelizability to simulate bidirectional attention without cost. The finding that this helps most for "options-first" formats suggests prompt engineering guides should reconsider how they structure multi-part queries. The custom tasks (NameIndex, MiddleMatch) starkly demonstrate the effect: models fail to retrieve positional information (25th name in a list) unless the list is repeated, suggesting positional embeddings in autoregressive models may not fully integrate with attention mechanisms.

## How Robust are LLMs to In-Context Majority Label Bias? (Gupta et al., 2023)

### Summary

1. Motivation: Prior work (Zhao et al. 2021) demonstrated that LLMs are susceptible to majority label bias—the tendency to predict labels that appear more frequently in few-shot examples. However, the robustness boundary of this susceptibility remained unquantified. The authors ask: under what distributional conditions does majority label bias actually degrade performance, and does model scale or instruction design mitigate it?

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike Zhao et al. who tested only extreme imbalance, this paper systematically varies label proportions across 11 settings (0%-100% true in 10% increments) on binary classification and 15 settings on multi-class. The authors introduce RB@K (Robustness Boundary)—the percentage of distribution settings where F1 stays within K% of peak performance—which captures practical tolerance for skewed data better than mean/variance metrics alone. The key innovation is quantifying *when* bias matters rather than showing *that* it exists.

3. Method: Experiments use 5 open-source LLMs (OpenLlama-7B/13B, MPT-7B/30B, Falcon-40B) instruction-tuned on OASST, evaluated on BoolQ (binary QA), RTE (binary entailment), and COVID-5G (3-way misinformation classification). For each dataset, N in-context examples are drawn with controlled label proportions. The authors vary label distributions systematically and compare "with instruction" vs "without instruction" prompt conditions. The RB@10 metric reports the fraction of settings where weighted F1 falls within 10% of maximum observed F1.

4. Results: Contrary to Zhao et al.'s "susceptibility" narrative, binary classification shows substantial robustness: RB@10 ranges from ~50-100% depending on model. Larger models achieve higher RB@10 (Falcon-40B with instruction: 90.9% on BoolQ, 100% on RTE). However, multi-class classification (COVID-5G) shows reduced robustness (RB@10 drops to 46.7-53.3% even for large models). Task-specific instructions substantially improve robustness at distribution tails—extreme skewness cases—with larger models benefiting more from instructions (~27.9% F1 improvement for 30B/40B models without instruction).

5. Significance: This paper reframes majority label bias as a *continuous* robustness question rather than a binary vulnerability. The finding that RB@K metrics can differ substantially from mean/variance (e.g., reversing model rankings) argues for robustness-specific evaluation in addition to standard metrics. Practical guidance: (a) binary classification tolerates skewed few-shot data better than multi-class; (b) larger models are more robust; (c) explicit task instructions matter most when in-context examples are highly imbalanced. The gap with multi-class suggests that label space complexity—not just imbalance ratio—is the limiting factor for robustness.
